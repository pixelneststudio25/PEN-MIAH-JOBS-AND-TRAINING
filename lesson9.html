Conflict Resolution

What Is Conflict Resolution?

Conflict resolution is the process by which two or more parties engaged in a disagreement, dispute, or debate reach an agreement that resolves it. It involves understanding the underlying interests, communicating effectively, and finding a mutually acceptable solution. Effective conflict resolution strengthens relationships, improves team dynamics, and saves time and resources.

Conflicts are inevitable in any organisation, but unresolved conflicts can lead to low morale, reduced productivity, and even reputational damage. Developing a mindset that sees conflict as an opportunity for growth is essential for leaders and team members alike.

Steps to the Conflict Resolution Process
  1. Clarify what the disagreement is. Ensure both parties articulate their perspectives without interruption. Ask open‑ended questions to uncover the root cause.
  2. Establish a common goal for both parties. Find shared interests – for example, “We both want the project to succeed.” This shifts focus from opposing positions to mutual benefit.
  3. Discuss ways to meet the common goal. Brainstorm possible solutions together. Encourage creativity and avoid judging ideas prematurely.
  4. Determine the barriers to the common goal. Identify obstacles – they could be resource constraints, miscommunication, or personal differences.
  5. Agree on the best way to resolve the conflict. Evaluate the options and choose one that addresses the key concerns of both parties.
  6. Keep it simple. Avoid overcomplicating the solution. A straightforward plan is easier to implement and maintain.

“Never ignore conflicts. Develop a mindset that loves to resolve conflict. Conflicts resolved are very profitable and can save your organisation lots of time, money, energy, and reputation.”

Five Conflict Handling Styles

Different situations call for different approaches. The Thomas‑Kilmann model outlines five common styles:

  • Competing: Assertive, uncooperative – used when quick, decisive action is needed (e.g., emergencies).
  • Collaborating: Assertive and cooperative – seeks a win‑win solution through open dialogue.
  • Compromising: Moderate assertiveness and cooperation – each party gives something up to reach a middle ground.
  • Avoiding: Unassertive, uncooperative – postponing or withdrawing from the conflict (useful when the issue is trivial).
  • Accommodating: Unassertive, cooperative – prioritising the relationship over personal goals.

Understanding these styles helps you choose the most effective approach for each situation.

Using a Mediator for Workplace Conflict Resolution

When parties cannot resolve a conflict themselves, a mediator can help. A mediator is a neutral third party who facilitates communication and guides the process without imposing a solution.

Using a mediator does not mean you have to employ someone external. An internal employee who is not involved in the conflict can work just as well – a manager, HR representative, business coach, or trusted co‑worker. The mediator must be impartial and trusted by both sides.

Tips for Successful Conflict Resolution
  • Listen actively: Let each person speak without interruption. Show you understand by paraphrasing.
  • Stay calm and respectful: Avoid raising your voice or using accusatory language.
  • Focus on interests, not positions: Ask “why” to uncover underlying needs.
  • Separate the person from the problem: Attack the issue, not the individual.
  • Be willing to apologise: If you’ve contributed to the conflict, a sincere apology can defuse tension.
  • Document the agreement: Write down the resolution and action steps to ensure accountability.
