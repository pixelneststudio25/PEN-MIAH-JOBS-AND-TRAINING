Communication Skills

What Is Communication?

Communication is the imparting or exchanging of information by speaking, writing, or using some other medium. It is the foundation of human interaction – in personal relationships, teams, and organisations. Effective communication ensures that messages are understood as intended, reducing errors, building trust, and fostering collaboration.

Five Types of Communication
  1. Verbal Communication – Spoken words in face‑to‑face conversations, phone calls, video meetings. Tone, clarity, and pace matter.
  2. Non‑verbal / Interpersonal Communication – Body language, facial expressions, gestures, eye contact, posture. Often conveys more than words.
  3. Written Communications – Emails, reports, messages, letters. Requires clarity, structure, and attention to grammar.
  4. Formal & Informal – Formal communication follows official channels (reports, presentations). Informal occurs in casual settings (hallway chats, social media).
  5. Visual Communication – Charts, graphs, images, videos. Helps simplify complex information and engage audiences.
The Communication Process

For communication to be effective, each element must work in harmony:

  • SENDER / ENCODER – The person who initiates the message and chooses how to convey it.
  • MEDIUM – The form of the message (e.g., speech, text, image).
  • CHANNEL – The pathway (e.g., face‑to‑face, email, phone).
  • RECEIVER – The person for whom the message is intended.
  • FEEDBACK – The receiver’s response, which confirms whether the message was understood.
  • CONTEXT – The environment, relationship, and cultural setting in which communication occurs.
  • NOISE (interference) – Anything that distorts or disrupts the message (e.g., background sound, language barriers, assumptions).
Communication Across Cultures

Different cultures have distinct communication norms – what is polite in one may be offensive in another. Be mindful of:

  • Direct vs. indirect speech: Some cultures value explicit statements; others prefer subtle hints.
  • Personal space: Proximity during conversation varies widely.
  • Non‑verbal cues: Gestures (e.g., thumbs up) can have different meanings.
  • Hierarchy and formality: Addressing people by titles vs. first names.
How to Improve Your Communication Skills
  • Listen actively: Focus on the speaker, avoid interrupting, and ask clarifying questions.
  • Be clear and concise: Organise your thoughts before speaking or writing.
  • Pay attention to non‑verbal signals: Maintain eye contact, watch your tone.
  • Adapt to your audience: Use language and examples they can relate to.
  • Ask for feedback: “Did I explain that clearly?” shows you care about understanding.
  • Practice empathy: Try to see things from the other person’s perspective.
  • Reduce noise: Choose a quiet setting for important conversations.
